Psychologists

Who are they?

A psychologist is a professional in psychology, a complex discipline that brings together different theoretical and practical currents, specifically: clinical, cognitive, behavioral, developmental, social, work, animal, differential and experimental psychology. These different fields of application are similar in relation to the study of psychic facts which is psychology.

Source

What do they do?

Psychologists help a wide variety of patients and can treat numerous types of problems. Some people consult a psychologist because they have been feeling depressed, angry, or anxious for a long time. Others want help with a chronic disorder that interferes with their lives or physical health. Still, others turn to a psychologist because they are experiencing short-term problems that they want to resolve, such as feeling overwhelmed by a new job or being affected by the death of a family member. Psychologists also help us to cope with stressful situations, heal from addictions, manage chronic illnesses and remove obstacles that prevent us from achieving our goals.

Source

How do they help us?

Psychologists use a variety of evidence-based treatments to help us improve our lives. They generally use therapy (often referred to as "psychotherapy" or "talk therapy"). Although there are different styles of therapy, the psychologist will choose the type that most effectively addresses the patient's problem, and best matches the patient's characteristics and preferences.

Types of anxiety

Anxiety symptoms are the psychiatric pathology with the highest prevalence rates in adolescents, affecting 9-21% of the general population. Fear and anxiety are normal defense reactions to environmental stress; however, when this reaction is disproportionate in intensity and frequency, it can be limiting the subject's daily activity, it becomes a psychiatric disorder. Anxiety disorders include the following types:

  • Separation anxiety
  • Generalized anxiety
  • Simple phobias, school phobia and social phobia
  • Obsessive-compulsive
  • Post-traumatic

Bipolar disorder

Bipolar disorder (BD) is a serious mental disorder, which affects more than 1% of worldwide population. Is characterized by extreme disturbance in mood alternating depression with mania. It is accompanied by emotional and cognitive disorders, as well as recurrent changes in mood and behavior. Its chronic and recurrent nature leads to functional and cognitive impairment, related with lower quality of life and higher mortality (mainly suicide and heart disease). In spite of appearing at early ages, therapeutic approach begins in adulthood, taking into account the time elapsed since the appearance of the first symptoms and the diagnosis.

Source

What is psychotherapy?

Psychotherapy is a collaborative treatment based on the relationship between a person and the psychologist. Its fundamental basis is dialogue, providing a supportive environment that allows you to speak openly with someone objective, neutral and impartial. In psychotherapy, psychologists apply scientifically valid procedures for the creation of healthier and more effective habits. Psychotherapy has several methods such as cognitive-behavioral, interpersonal and other types of talk therapy that help solve problems.

Source

How does psychotherapy help people recover from depression?

There are several approaches to psychotherapy, including cognitive-behavioral, interpersonal, psychodynamic, and other kinds of talk therapy, that help people recover from depression. Psychotherapy offers the opportunity to identify the factors contributing to your depression and to effectively address the psychological, behavioral, interpersonal, and situational causes.

Source

When should psychotherapy be considered as an option?

It is possible that, due to the existence of numerous misconceptions about psychotherapy, you may be reluctant to consider psychotherapy. Even if you know the facts and not the myths, you are probably nervous about considering such an approach to your problem. But it's worth overcoming your nervousness because, if at some point your quality of life is not what you want it to be, psychotherapy can help.
